Before this module, I assumed that as AI models get more advanced it will gradually reduce implicit bias. However, the 2025 AI index report challenges my assumption. It clarifies that as foundation models scale up with massive data their implicit biases can actually increase, even if their performance on standard benchmarks improve. In addition, the Bommasani et al. (2021) paper emphasizes that while scale enables impressive emergent capabilities, it also stretches the potential to accentuate harms that are currently poorly understood. This further challenges my assumption that more advanced AI systems will become inherently fairer, because scaling may embed societal stereotypes more deeply into a foundation model’s underlying mechanisms.

I was surprised to learn from the 2025 AI index report that LLMs continue to struggle with complex reasoning. Even as they scale and demonstrate impressive emergent capabilities, LLMs still cannot reliably solve problems for which provably correct solutions can be found using logical reasoning, such as arithmetic and planning. This matters for my professional work as a financial accounting researcher and educator because financial accounting demands absolute precision and reliability. Researchers and students analyzing complex financial reporting and disclosures cannot assume a model is mathematically or logically reliable simply because it is larger or more advanced. This technical limitation necessitates rigorous human oversight to prevent AI hallucinations from distorting financial analysis or market signals.

Based on what I learned, researchers are increasingly using foundation models, such as GPT-4 and Claude, which leverage self-supervised learning to perform complex language generation tasks.
